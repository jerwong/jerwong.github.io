Challenge:

The BMW M3 is an iconic car in the automotive world. It started back in 1986 when the M Division created the legendary E30 M3 that won championships across the world. Fast forward 30 years and 4 generations later, we have the latest interpretation of the M3.

In honour of this great achievement, I've decided to explore each generation of the M3 — taking the audience on a journey of what made each car truly special.

The challenge was to represent the fundamental characteristics of each generation in order to tell the story of the M3’s evolution in a universal matter with minimal words.


Response:

Started by researching the overall characteristics/trademark features of the M3, which was its chassis balance, precise control, and the metallic rasp of an inline 6 cylinder engine. Based on this concept, I created a set of icons to represent different aspects of the car. The concept is that when the reader looks at the group of icons, they should have a general understanding of the car’s personality. As such, I tried to create icons that would be easily interpreted from first glance. Some aspects were more difficult to represent, such as the “metallic rasp” of the engine note, or the “bland burble” of the new turbocharged engine. Due to the abstract nature of some of the characteristics, it was impossible to create unique icons without different meanings being associated with them.

Other key elements such as the body profile, fascia, and engines serve to represent the vehicle’s physical changes. The size of the cars play an important role in the story as well. Each generation becomes more aerodynamic and aggressive, but also longer. The dual “angel eye” headlights have always been an iconic symbol of BMWs.

The primary target audience are car enthusiasts and potential sports car buyers. The infographic serves as an educational tool online for them or as a nice poster on the wall in tribute of the M3.


Outcome:

This project was a lot of fun, but very challenging. When I started it, I thought it was going to be simple, but as the concept evolved (heh), it really started to challenge my thinking. It was a good lesson in problem solving and compromise.

I user tested the project with many people that were not in the target audience (non-car people) to determine the success of the concept. Most users were able to understand the basic character of the cars, but some icons were more effective than others.
